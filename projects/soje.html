Remember: Soje

Silence tour & Photowalk workshop
Nov. 23

Soje-dong (소제동) is an old railroad village behind Daejeon station. The entire area was on the verge of disappearing after the local government marked the area for urban development and people deserted the area. But now its reputation is totally different from the past.

The history of Soje-dong traces back to the Japanese occupation of Korea (1910-1945). At the time, an important railroad connected the granaries of Gunsan to Seoul, and Soje-dong was created in the middle of it to be the official management office/town. The area was previously occupied by a beautiful lake, “Sojeho lake” (소제호), filled by the Japanese army. The village developed into a prosperous area where many Japanese-style houses were built as the neighborhood grew. However, after the end of the Japanese colonial occupation, many of these houses were left unattended, labelled of “Jeoksan houses” (적산가옥), negative heritage, and seen as a reminder of a painful time. As such, the rich village, where the Japanese army once lived, turned into an area where people were reluctant to live in. The local government announced a plan to rebuild the entire neighborhood and people left one by one. The development plan never happened and the area turned into a slum.

For some, it is just a neighborhood, but I am personally connected to it. It is the place where my father had to work from the young age of 14, due to poverty. He still hates that area. But to me, as a 20-year-old with a passion for photography, Soje-dong was a diamond in the rough, and, despite my father’s disapproval, I visited it three times a week. The Daejeon city government made various attempts to revigorate the neighborhood, such as offering artist residencies, commissioning mural paintings, and trying to turn it into an attractive cultural neighborhood. All the attempts failed, and urban redevelopment threatened once more the old village.

I did not want to let this town disappear. It was where I practiced photography since the first time I held a camera and the place that made me understand my usually silent father. I could not let this neighborhood be forgotten, and that is why I started this project. I met with a startup CEO, who held local marketing events, and shared my ideas with him on how to show the true value of Soje-dong. In collaboration with an event company, I designed a Silent Tour to promote the area. I would guide people wearing headphones, telling them the history of the village, opening up about my father’s story, playing nostalgic music, and reading poetry, while showing them all the secret views and hidden gems of Soje-dong. I became the docent and guardian of this old neighborhood. We walked these streets where you can feel the weight of time and captured these anachronic moments with my film camera. At the end of the tour, I would lead them to a local café located in an old bed and breakfast from the Japanese colonial time, reflecting on the experience and the new perspective on the neighborhood.

These days, with the resurgent interest in vintage and retro-aesthetic, many turned their attention toward Soje-dong and what was left of its historical buildings. Unfortunately, this attention is superficial and buildings have been indiscriminately turned into cafés and gifts shops without care, damaging the buildings with condensation and mold. This newfound interest is propelled by private companies, not grass-root or local movements, pushing for gentrification and short term-profit. Therefore, the neighborhood is endangered once again, as it is getting squeezed of its value, and may turn back into a slum as trendy cafés already starts to closes as fast as they opened. Soje-dong survives on a fine balance. From a complicated history, to governmental mismanagement and damaging attention due to trends, how can we protect this neighborhood and coexist with the community?
