Trigger: After entering university in Japan, I felt strange on a very bright morning. Since it was my first time living abroad, I did not have the chance to be conscious of the time difference. Since Korea has a history of invasions and wars, even the time to enjoy the sun has been controlled according to the needs and ideology of neighboring countries. This time difference persists to this day.

Solution: Whenever I felt anxious while living abroad alone at a young age, I ironically found out that it was the 'light' that relieved my tension. I made a video of such moments of gratitude.

Surprisingly, it feels like Japan has longer days than Korea. It is something I experienced living there for over 8 years. However, when sharing my experience, people cannot believe it to be true, stating that Japan and Korea are close and share the same time zone.

Latitude of Korea: 127.5°
Latitude of Japan: 135°

Therefore, it would be normal for Korea and Japan to have a 30 minutes time difference. Korean timezone varied quite a lot over the course of history. In particular In 1912, due to the Japanese colonial occupation of Korea, standard time was adjusted to match Japanese time. This was reversed in 1954, 9 years after Korea independence, but moved again in 1961. This was a strategic decision made by the US to operate more conveniently in the area. Therefore, even something as pure as the sun rising in the morning is heavy from the weight of history and Korean morning are darker than they should be.

I was living in Japan and could enjoy warmth and comfort from the morning sun, but I also felt uncomfortable thinking about history. Nevertheless, I captured the moment I felt grateful for the light and turned it in a video collage.
