Throughout my career, I held various titles such as content marketer or brand manager. By taking advantage of my long stay in Japan, I could work to implant diverse Korean products, such as sportswear, service, and applications, in the Japanese market. Among those experiences, working for “MindCafe” made me think really deeply about the true meaning of “service” and responsible advertisement.

In East Asia, psychiatric counseling is taboo due to the conservative social climate. As social expectations are higher and fiercer than anywhere else, many people fall through the cracks of the system or hide themselves and do not receive the mental help they direly need.

The service “MindCafe” provides non-face-to-face professional support to people in need of psychiatric counseling and treatment. It first started as an online community where people could open their minds and talk about their feelings and then it started to provide professional help to those in need. The service started in South Korea and planned to expand to Japan, recognizing the same symptoms and need for discreet and comfortable professional psychiatric support. I joined at the start of this project, bringing my professional skills but also my deep understanding of Japanese society.

Working for MindCafe made me want to learn more deeply about visual and communication. Marketing was essential to reach more people and help them, but the message I wanted to convey was too abstract to attract people’s attention. I had no choice but to use stimulating images and strong catchphrases that would win people’s clicks, but I did not think these intense stimulations matched the service we provided.

Banners for marketing campaign. Click to enlarge.

“I am lonely but there is no one to open up to.”
“The feeling that I cannot tell my family.”
“I make mistakes one after another. Adult ADHD?”

Using such phrases that amplify people’s insecurity and anxiety increased the number of users on the platform, but it felt like it was achieved by feeding their fears. While I continued to aggressively expand this marketing campaign, I tried to bring balance by building brand content more in line with the true values of Mind Cafe. Such content was kinder, not triggering, and more supportive and patient. But the contrast between the aggressive marketing campaigns and the positive branding content continued to grow. It gave me an eerie and uneasy feeling, and it felt that I was doing none of them right.

This experience made me think about conscious marketing and how we can reach people in a healthy way, rather than resorting to violent and loud messages to get quick short-term engagement.

"Mamorime" service design

For Japanese market, team and I designed service name "mamorime" which is provide professional help for user who left post on community in random. The word "mamorime" containing meaning of "guard something" in Japanese, 守る（マモル）. To express the feature well I come up with idea, combining heart and guardian angel.
